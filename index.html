Part I · The Challenge

Compliance today is broken into pieces.

Most firms operating in the UAE manage compliance across a stack of disconnected tools — each with a separate login, separate data, separate audit trail. The result is risk that hides between the seams.

Fragmented Stacks
KYC vendor here. Transaction monitor there. AML screening somewhere else. Reports stitched together in spreadsheets.
Manual Workflows
MLRO approvals on email. SARs typed by hand. Travel Rule chased through spreadsheets — delaying transactions and inviting error.
Regulatory Exposure
Six UAE regulators with overlapping but distinct rules. One missed obligation, one weak audit trail, can end a license.
The UAE deserves a single source of truth for compliance.
Part II · The Platform

Introducing ComplianceX.

The UAE's first multi-regulator compliance operating system. KYC, transaction monitoring, AML screening, Travel Rule, and MLRO workflows — consolidated into one platform, with one audit trail, designed specifically for UAE regulators.

  • One platform for the entire compliance lifecycle.
  • Six UAE regulators mapped out of the box.
  • Pre-templated rules tuned for UAE obligations.
  • Immutable audit trail with eight-year retention.

GL Score Basics

The GL Score is a number from 0 to 100 that indicates the risk level of a blockchain address or transaction.

0 – 29
LOW RISK
30 – 69
MEDIUM RISK
70 – 100
HIGH RISK

The score is calculated using a risk-based approach aligned with FATF (Financial Action Task Force) guidelines. It considers four pillars:

  • Entity risk — the inherent risk of the entity the address belongs to.
  • Source of funds — where the money came from (traced backwards).
  • Use of funds — where the money went (traced forwards).
  • Address risk — direct tags/labels on the address (sanctions, darknet, etc.).

These four factors are combined using volume weighting, meaning sources with more volume have more impact. However, a risk flag mechanism ensures that even tiny exposure to sanctioned entities elevates the score significantly.

🏛️

Entity Risk Categories

Every known entity on the blockchain is classified into a category and assigned an inherent risk score. This is the entity risk — it reflects the nature of the service, not the specific transaction.

CategoryTypical ScoreWhy
Regulated exchange● 0-20KYC-verified, regulated, audited
Mining pool● 0-10Freshly mined coins carry no prior risk
DeFi protocol● 20-40Mixed, pseudonymous users
Unregulated exchange● 30-50No KYC, limited compliance
Gambling● 40-60Potential money laundering vector
Mixer / tumbler● 70-90Designed to obscure fund origin
Darknet market● 90-100Illegal goods and services
Sanctioned entity● 100OFAC, EU, or UN sanctioned

A sanctioned entity will always push risk to the maximum. Even if only 0.1% of funds pass through a sanctioned address, the risk flag triggers.

Lesson 3 of 6
🔎

Source & Use of Funds

The algorithm traces funds in two directions:

← SOURCE OF FUNDS (INCOMING)

Traces backwards through incoming transactions to identify where money originated. Follows the chain until it reaches an identified entity, smart contract, or unhosted wallet.

USE OF FUNDS (OUTGOING) →

Traces forwards through outgoing transactions to identify where money was sent. Reveals if funds end up at darknet markets, sanctioned entities, etc.

Depth & Share

Each hop back or forward is called a depth level:

  • Depth 0 — Direct counterparty (sent/received directly).
  • Depth 1–3 — Close proximity, still high influence on score.
  • Depth 4+ — Indirect; influence diminishes with distance.

Each identified source also has a share — the percentage of total volume it represents. A source with 60% share has much more impact than one with 2%.

Volume-Weighted Formula

GL Score ≈ Σ (source_risk × volume_share) + risk_flag_penalties

This means the final score is the sum of each source's risk score multiplied by its share of total volume, plus any additional penalties for flagged entities.

Lesson 4 of 6
🚩

Risk Flags & Alerts

The GL Score includes a risk flag mechanism that ensures certain categories always elevate the score, even with minimal volume:

Sanctions (OFAC, EU, UN)
Any link to a sanctioned entity triggers maximum alert.
Terrorism financing
Addresses linked to known terrorist organizations.
Darknet markets
Illegal marketplaces for drugs, weapons, stolen data.
Ransomware / Cybercrime
Known ransomware payment wallets.
Mixers & tumblers
Services designed to hide fund origins.
Child exploitation (CSAM)
Zero-tolerance, always maximum severity.

Why this matters: A wallet might show a GL Score of 35 (medium), but if one of its sources is a sanctioned entity — even at 0.5% volume — a risk flag is triggered. Compliance officers should always check the Alerts section alongside the score.

Key takeaway: The numerical score alone is not enough. Always review risk flags and alerts before making a compliance decision.
Lesson 5 of 6
⚖️

Compliance Decision Making

After reviewing a GL Score report, compliance officers must decide on the appropriate action. The decision framework aligns with FATF recommendations:

LOW (0–29) → STANDARD PROCESSING
  • Proceed with standard Customer Due Diligence (CDD)
  • Document the score and source analysis
  • No escalation needed (unless risk flags present)
MEDIUM (30–69) → ENHANCED DUE DILIGENCE
  • Perform Enhanced Due Diligence (EDD)
  • Review source and use of funds breakdown in detail
  • Request additional documentation from customer
  • Escalate to MLRO if risk flags are present
HIGH (70–100) → BLOCK & ESCALATE
  • Immediately flag for compliance review
  • Consider blocking the transaction
  • File a Suspicious Activity Report (SAR)
  • Notify the Money Laundering Reporting Officer (MLRO)

Key terms:

  • CDD — Customer Due Diligence: standard identity and risk checks.
  • EDD — Enhanced Due Diligence: deeper investigation for higher-risk cases.
  • SAR — Suspicious Activity Report: filed with law enforcement.
  • MLRO — Money Laundering Reporting Officer: designated compliance lead.
  • PEP — Politically Exposed Person: individuals with political influence.
Lesson 6 of 6
📋

Reading a Risk Report

When you run an address or transaction lookup, the report contains several key sections. Here's how to read each one:

  1. GL Score Gauge — The large circular gauge at the top. Gives you an instant triage: green (safe), yellow (review), red (escalate).
  2. Highest Risk Flag — Displayed above the gauge. Shows the entity with the highest individual risk score. If this is a sanctioned entity, treat with maximum caution.
  3. Source vs Use of Funds — Left side shows where money came from, right side shows where it went. Each entity is labeled with a risk badge.
  4. Dominant Entity — Below the gauge. This is the entity with the largest volume share — the wallet's primary counterparty.
  5. Key Metrics — Analysis Depth (how many hops traced), Progress (% of funds identified), Volume (total USD analyzed).
  6. Sources Table — Detailed breakdown of every identified source with individual risk scores, shares, and depth. Sort by score to find the riskiest.
  7. Alerts Table — Specific risk triggers with their own scores and associated addresses.
  8. Profile Section — Wallet type classification, entity labels, compliance status (Blocked/Not Blocked), known tags.
Pro tip: Always compare the Dominant Entity with the Highest Risk Flag. If they're different, it means the wallet mostly transacts with a low-risk entity but has some exposure to a high-risk one — a common pattern that requires EDD.
